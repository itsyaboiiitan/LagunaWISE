The Highlands of Laguna

Laguna is a sanctuary where the earth meets the sky, defined by a skyline of emerald peaks and a heart of ancient, mossy forests. Beyond the urban sprawl lies a world of breathtaking biodiversity and highland adventures that cater to both the soul-seeker and the adrenaline-chaser.

  • Mount Makiling: A dormant volcano with a moderately challenging trail that winds through lush forests, offering panoramic views of Laguna de Bay and surrounding provinces.
  • Mount Banahaw: Discover a mystical sanctuary of serenity where sacred peaks meet restorative hot springs for the ultimate soul-soothing escape.
  • Mount Romelo: Known for its gentle trails and captivating waterfalls. Often referred to as the “Falls Capital of Laguna,” the mountain is home to several stunning cascades, including Buruwisan, Batya-Batya, and Lanzones Falls, each offering crystal-clear pools perfect for swimming and relaxation.
  • Mount Kalisungan: A favorite among beginner hikers and nature enthusiasts seeking a rewarding yet manageable adventure. Standing at 760 meters above sea level, this mountain offers a straightforward trail surrounded by rolling grasslands, coconut plantations, and patches of forest.
  • Mount Mabilog: known for its biodiversity, with lush greenery and vibrant wildlife accompanying you along the trail. Whether you’re looking for a quick day hike or a peaceful retreat into nature, Mount Mabilog is an ideal destination that showcases Laguna’s natural charm without requiring an intense climb.
  • Mount Cristobal: A nicely shaped conic volcano covered by dense rainforest. The forests here are said to be one of the most pristine in Luzon. However, there are many legends based on the “negative energy” surrounding this peak that may either appeal to or spook off the average hiker.

Local Biodiversity Spotlight

Step into our local wild. Learn about the unique flora and fauna native to our region, such as the rare Rafflesia and the Philippine Hanging Parrot. Discover how these endemic species maintain the ecological balance of Laguna's ecosystems.

  • Rafflesia & Jade Vine: Regarded as rare and threatened, these iconic plants are found in the lake basin's remaining forested peaks.
  • Philippine Hanging Parrot (Loriculus philippensis): A vibrant master of the forest canopy that serves as a vital pollinator and seed spreader for native trees. Locally named "Kulasisi".
  • Endemic Bird Species: Laguna holds 15 globally threatened bird species and serves as a critical sanctuary for migratory water birds.
  • Unique Aquatic Life: The lake is home to indigenous fish like the Silver Perch (Bidyanus bidyanus) or Ayungin and the Dwarf Pygmy Goby (Pandaka pygmaea), one of the world's smallest freshwater fish.
  • Ecological Stewardship: These species maintain a healthy food web and regulate our air and water quality, protecting the resilient ecosystems of Laguna.

Laguna de Bay

Laguna de Bay is the largest inland body of water in the Philippines and a critical lifeblood for the Calabarzon region. Spanning approximately 900 square kilometers, it serves as a multi-purpose resource for millions.

  • Economic Anchor: The lake supports a massive aquaculture industry, providing a primary livelihood for over 13,000 fisherfolk and acting as a major source of freshwater fish for Metro Manila.
  • Vital Infrastructure: Beyond fishing, the lake is essential for flood control, irrigation for surrounding farmlands, and industrial cooling.
  • Power & Energy: It contributes significantly to the national grid through hydropower generation, producing roughly 700 megawatts of electricity.
  • Ecological Importance: As a semi-enclosed basin, it hosts a unique mix of freshwater and marine-influenced species, including the endemic Ayungin.
  • Sustainability Focus: Current management strategies aim to balance these intense human uses with environmental protection to ensure the lake's long-term water quality and biodiversity.
